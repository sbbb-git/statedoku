COColorado History

Capital: Denver · West · Admitted 1876

Colorado at a glance

Colorado was admitted to the Union on 1876, making it the 38th state. It joined the country 100 years after the Declaration of Independence. Its capital Denver has been the seat of state government for most of the state's history.

Native peoples

For thousands of years before European contact, Indigenous peoples lived across what is now Colorado. Multiple distinct cultures developed in response to the region's geography — coastal peoples, plains peoples, river-valley peoples, mountain peoples — each with their own languages, governance, and trade networks. European colonization disrupted these civilizations dramatically, often through disease, displacement, and forced removal. Today, Colorado's Native heritage is preserved in place names, tribal nations within state borders, and ongoing cultural practices.

Spanish colonial era

Colorado carries a deep Spanish colonial legacy. Spanish missionaries, conquistadors, and settlers reached the region in the 16th and 17th centuries — earlier than English colonization elsewhere. The legacy survives in place names (cities, rivers, missions), architecture, cuisine, and the Hispanic and Latino communities that have lived continuously in the area for over four centuries. Many of the oldest US cities west of the Mississippi were originally Spanish.

The Louisiana Purchase (1803)

Colorado was part of the territory the United States acquired from France in 1803 for $15 million — the Louisiana Purchase, which doubled the size of the country overnight. The purchase set in motion decades of westward exploration and settlement, including the Lewis and Clark Expedition (1804-1806).

The Mexican Cession (1848)

Colorado came to the United States as part of the 1848 Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo, which ended the Mexican-American War. The cession transferred a vast region including what would become California, Nevada, Utah, most of Arizona and New Mexico, and parts of Colorado, Kansas, and Wyoming. The 1854 Gadsden Purchase finalized the current US-Mexico border in the south.

Path to statehood

The path from European exploration to statehood typically took decades. Colorado was admitted to the Union on 1876, after meeting the requirements set by Congress: sufficient population, a written constitution, and the consent of Congress. The process from initial settlement to statehood was the 90th time the US expanded.

The modern era

Today Colorado has a population of 5 to 10 million and is one of the largest states in the country. The capital is Denver; the largest city is Denver. The economy benefits significantly from oil and natural gas production. Tourism is a major economic driver. The political lean has shifted over time and now leans reliably Democratic.

Common questions about Colorado history

When did Colorado become a state?

Colorado became a state in 1876, making it the 38th state admitted to the Union.

Was Colorado part of the Confederacy?

No, Colorado was not part of the Confederacy.

Was Colorado part of the Louisiana Purchase?

Yes, Colorado was acquired by the US as part of the 1803 Louisiana Purchase from France.

Did Spain or France colonize Colorado?

Yes, Spanish colonization shaped early Colorado — visible today in place names, architecture, and culture.
