DEDelaware History

Capital: Dover · Northeast · Admitted 1787

Delaware at a glance

Delaware was admitted to the Union on 1787, making it the 1st state. As one of the original 13 colonies, Delaware was a founding member of the United States. Its capital Dover has been the seat of state government for most of the state's history.

Native peoples

For thousands of years before European contact, Indigenous peoples lived across what is now Delaware. Multiple distinct cultures developed in response to the region's geography — coastal peoples, plains peoples, river-valley peoples, mountain peoples — each with their own languages, governance, and trade networks. European colonization disrupted these civilizations dramatically, often through disease, displacement, and forced removal. Today, Delaware's Native heritage is preserved in place names, tribal nations within state borders, and ongoing cultural practices.

Colonial era and Revolution

As one of the 13 original colonies, Delaware was central to the founding of the United States. British colonization of the region began in the 17th century. By the 1770s, colonial assemblies were resisting British taxation policies, and Delaware sent delegates to the Continental Congress that declared independence in 1776. Delaware fought in the Revolutionary War alongside the other 12 colonies and ratified the US Constitution between 1787 and 1790, depending on the specific date.

Path to statehood

The path from European exploration to statehood typically took decades. Delaware was already an established colony when the US declared independence in 1776, becoming a state with the ratification of the US Constitution by 1787.

The modern era

Today Delaware has a population of 1 to 5 million and is one of the smallest states in the country. The capital is Dover; the largest city is Wilmington. The political lean has shifted over time and now leans reliably Democratic.
