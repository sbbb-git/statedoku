Capital: Sacramento · West · Admitted 1850

California at a glance

California was admitted to the Union on 1850, making it the 31st state. It joined the country 74 years after the Declaration of Independence. Its capital Sacramento has been the seat of state government for most of the state's history.

Native peoples

For thousands of years before European contact, Indigenous peoples lived across what is now California. Multiple distinct cultures developed in response to the region's geography — coastal peoples, plains peoples, river-valley peoples, mountain peoples — each with their own languages, governance, and trade networks. European colonization disrupted these civilizations dramatically, often through disease, displacement, and forced removal. Today, California's Native heritage is preserved in place names, tribal nations within state borders, and ongoing cultural practices.

Spanish colonial era

California carries a deep Spanish colonial legacy. Spanish missionaries, conquistadors, and settlers reached the region in the 16th and 17th centuries — earlier than English colonization elsewhere. The legacy survives in place names (cities, rivers, missions), architecture, cuisine, and the Hispanic and Latino communities that have lived continuously in the area for over four centuries. Many of the oldest US cities west of the Mississippi were originally Spanish.

The Mexican Cession (1848)

California came to the United States as part of the 1848 Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo, which ended the Mexican-American War. The cession transferred a vast region including what would become California, Nevada, Utah, most of Arizona and New Mexico, and parts of Colorado, Kansas, and Wyoming. The 1854 Gadsden Purchase finalized the current US-Mexico border in the south.

Path to statehood

The path from European exploration to statehood typically took decades. California was admitted to the Union on 1850, after meeting the requirements set by Congress: sufficient population, a written constitution, and the consent of Congress. The process from initial settlement to statehood was the 64th time the US expanded.

The modern era

Today California has a population of over 10 million and is one of the largest states in the country. The capital is Sacramento; the largest city is Los Angeles. The economy benefits significantly from oil and natural gas production. Agriculture remains an important sector of the state economy. Tourism is a major economic driver. The political lean has shifted over time and now leans reliably Democratic.
